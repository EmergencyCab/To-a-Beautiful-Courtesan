एक सुन्दरी वेश्या प्रति

To a Beautiful Courtesan

Laxmi Prasad Devkota  ·  1909–1959

written in the 1940s · Panchachamar chhanda

by Prashant Panta

❦

Introduction

Laxmi Prasad Devkota (1909 to 1959), whom Nepal calls Mahakavi, the Great Poet, wrote this poem in the 1940s as a direct rebuttal to Bhanubhakta Acharya's Badhu Shiksha, a moralistic poem that had become a cornerstone of respectable Nepali literary culture. Where Bhanubhakta had declared that any woman who laughed freely in public was a prostitute, Devkota found a courtesan's laughter genuinely captivating and philosophically significant. The argument between these two poems is, in miniature, the argument between two entirely different conceptions of the world: one that polices beauty in the name of order, and one that worships beauty as the most honest form of order that exists. As the Kathmandu Post noted of Bhushita Vasistha's celebrated narration of this poem, Devkota wrote it as a rebuttal of the aadikavi's Badhu Shiksha, and for Devkota, the world had become materialistic, with consumerism putting a price on everything, and humans failing to appreciate beauty and love.

The poem is written in the Panchachamar chhanda, a classical Sanskrit meter with a driving, insistent rhythm that Bhushita Vasistha herself has called her favourite among all the classical works she recites. Her narration, accompanied by harmonium, keyboard, flute, guitar and percussion, has brought the poem to a new generation of Nepali listeners who might otherwise have encountered it only on a page. The meter itself enacts the poem's argument: it does not pause, it does not apologise, it does not ask permission. It moves forward with the confidence of something that knows it is right.

Nepal in the 1940s was a country sealed inside itself by the Rana oligarchy, a system of hereditary prime ministers who had been running the country as private property since 1846. The ruling class celebrated its acquisitions while the vast majority of Nepali life unfolded in quiet deprivation, in a country where access to education, medicine, and legal protection was determined entirely by one's proximity to power. Into this context, the figure of the courtesan is not merely a personal subject for Devkota. She is a mirror held up to the entire arrangement. She is what happens when a society that claims to value beauty and purity applies a price to both.

Devkota sees a woman whom society calls fallen and asks a question that the poem never stops asking: how did someone so beautiful, sensitive, and alive end up being sold in the marketplace? Instead of condemning her, he condemns the society that turned a human being into a commodity. One of the recurring feelings in the poem is astonishment. How are you here, in the market? He sees her not as a prostitute but as a flower, as a work of creation, as a human soul. The shock comes from the contrast between her intrinsic worth and the circumstances she has been pushed into, and Devkota never lets that shock settle into acceptance. He wages the entire poem against the acceptance.

This document presents the full poem in Nepali and English side by side, followed by a verse by verse philosophical analysis that quotes the original lines and examines what Devkota was doing with them. That analysis draws on Hindu philosophical concepts including Shakti, rasa theory, the divine feminine, and the figures of Ambika, Urvashi, Menaka, Parvati, Radha, and Kali as they appear in the poem. It draws on the Romantic literary tradition including Keats and the equation of beauty and truth. And it draws on the social and political context of Rana-era Nepal, while never losing sight of the fact that this poem speaks to our own time with an urgency that has not diminished.

The Complete Poem · Nepali and English Side by Side

Original Nepali sourced from paleti.com.np and inepal.org. English translation by Prashant Panta. Bhushita Vasistha’s narration brought this poem to a new generation of Nepali listeners.

1
छ प्राप्तिको सुरा शरद् ! सुखोत्सवी छ मानव ! फली, फुकी, धनीधरा सुनौलिएर मुस्किइन्! सुधा–उरीज सप्किंदी ! स्वभावमस्त ! मुस्कुरी ! ए सुन्दरी ? रसा सरी रुमाल फर्फराउँछयौ ?
Autumn is the wine of achievement! Mankind is festive with pleasure! The rich earth blossomed, bloomed, gilded itself and smiled! Nectar-drops sparkling! Nature-intoxicated! Smiling! O beautiful one — do you flutter your handkerchief like the earth herself?
2
रसाल भार रेशमी विचित्र चित्र रेखिए ! बन्यौ कि फूलले तिमी ? सुगन्ध ली हवा बहे ! रङ्गीन ज्यान झ्यालकी ! बजारबीचकी उषा ! उपास्दथें म अम्बिका ! झसङ्ग झैं यहाँ भएँ !
Your silken burden is woven with strange and gorgeous patterns! Were you made of flowers? The wind carries your fragrance away! Colourful soul at the window! Dawn in the middle of the marketplace! I used to worship Ambika the goddess — now I am startled to find her here!
3
शरन्निशा तिमी झुकी, झिकी लजालु घूँघट, स्मिति–प्रयोगिनी बनी, जिती सफेद चाँदनी, सितारिंदी उदाउँछयौ ? बनाइ अङ्ग मख्मली ? टुना गरेर चित्तमा चकोरका झिलीमिली ?
In the autumn night you bow, pulling aside your shy veil, Becoming a practitioner of smiles, outshining the white moonlight, Do you rise scattering stars? Having made your limbs like velvet? Casting a spell in the heart with the glittering of moonbirds?
4
अनङ्गबाण झैं बस्यौ ? मिलिन्द डाक्न चञ्चली ! स्वरुप–शिल्पकारको टुना गवाउनाकन ? म हूँ सितार स्नायुको, बजीरहेछु झन्किँदै ! समुद्र आज हो बडो छचल्किँदो जुनेलिँदै !
Did you settle like Cupid's arrow? Restless one, to summon the bee! To make the architect of beauty himself sing his own enchantment? I am the sitar of nerves, playing and resonating! The ocean today is vast, churning and moonlit!
5
गुलाफतापले रचे विरिञ्चिले मनुष्य कि ? मिलेर राग, वासना, फलिन्, फुलिन् वसुन्धरा ! छ मासुरङ्ग रेशमी लुगा लगाउने चरी ! अनङ्गबागमा बसी अलाप्दछे धनी सुरा!
Did Brahma fashion mankind with the warmth of roses? Blending passion and desire, the earth bore fruit and blossomed! She is a bird wearing silken clothes the colour of flesh! Sitting in the garden of Desire, she sings her rich melody!
6
जिती समाधि साधुको परी अपूर्व छिल्लिए ! छ मृत्तिका–प्रधान नै मनुष्यजीव, भूसुता ! म मासु–लाञ्छना गरी म पस्छु है चिसो गुफा ! म फूलको हूँ पारखी ! म पुज्छु सृष्टि, सुन्दरी!
A fairy surpassing the saint's own meditation, she radiates wonder! The human soul is made primarily of clay, O daughter of earth! I enter this cold cave bearing the stigma of flesh! I am a connoisseur of flowers! I worship creation, O beautiful one!
7
मिहीन वाण हानिंदा, न घाउ लाग्नु हो शिला ! म मासुको ढुकढुकी हुँ! म वेदना, म शास हुँ ! म चर्म रे ! म दृष्टि रे म रश्मिको जवाफ हुँ ! म हारको विहार रे ! म पादको सिँगार हुँ !
When a fine arrow is shot, it does not wound a stone! I am the heartbeat of flesh! I am pain, I am breath! I am skin! I am vision! I am the answer to light! I am the wandering of a garland! I am the adornment of feet!
8
म स्वप्निंदो छु उर्वशी, जिलाउँदो छु मेनका ! मलाई दिव्य कामिनीको तिर्सना अनन्त छ ! म मत्तमा ! म मस्तता ! मलाई प्यास सुन्दर ! म अड्छु ! फुल्छु, हेर्छु रे ! मनुष्य पुष्प फर्फर !
I am the dreaming Urvashi, I am the life-giving Menaka! My longing for the divine feminine is endless! I am intoxication! I am ecstasy! I thirst for beauty! I stand firm! I bloom, I gaze! A human flower, fluttering!
9
इशार, फुल्छ चित्त यो ! पुकार, चल्छ छाति यो ! म मासुको गुलाफको मिठास के गरी? भूलूँ निसर्गको छ ऐन यो ! छ कृष्णलाई चैन यो ! समाधि–मग्न शम्भुको प्रकोपवादको खुशी !
At a gesture, this heart blooms! At a call, this chest stirs! How can I forget the sweetness of flesh's rose? This is nature's own law! This is Krishna's own peace! The joy of Shambhu's fury even in deepest meditation!
10
म दीपको पतङ्गको अडान–निम्ति वार के ! यही त ब्रह्मपाश हो ! र बाँधिने मिठास हो ! यही चकोर प्यास हो ! हुनाको मात, आश हो ! म पौडिने तरङ्ग हो ! म मस्तिने सुवास हो !
What barrier stands before the moth's devotion to the flame? This is Brahma's own snare! And the sweetness of being bound! This is the moonbird's thirst! The intoxication and hope of existence! I am the wave that swims! I am the fragrance that intoxicates!
11
न साँझ चान्द्र शैलमा प्रभापुरी अघि स्फुटी गुलाफिइन् हिमालका समस्त टाकुरा जली ! यही अपूर्व कीर्तिले ! पखेरु खल्बल्याउँदै ! इशार स्वर्ग ! छैन त्यो भनेर भो म भन्दिन !
Before the city of light had bloomed in the evening mountains, All the Himalayan peaks burned rose-coloured! By this extraordinary glory, while birds burst into commotion! A gesture toward heaven — I will not say that does not exist!
12
चिनेचिने सरी छ क्यै ! मलाई “आ” भनीकन इशार्दछयौ ! म आउँला ! पिएर तृप्तिँ ल्यूअघि ! मलाई बोधिसत्वमा पु¥याउँ भन्छ पालुवा ! समस्त चारु चीजले मलाई आज डाक्दछन्!
Something feels familiar! Beckoning me saying “come” — You gesture to me! I will come — drinking deep before satisfaction! The tendril says — lead me to Bodhisattva! All beautiful things are calling to me today!
13
तथापि अड्छु गौडले ! छ पर्श कालको यहाँ जहाँ उडेर रङ्ग क्यै परेछ दाग द्धारमा ! तिमी गिज्याउँछयौ अरे ! म मूर्ख हैन, ए पख ! समाजलज्ज मान्नको छ देखिने अरु टक !
Yet I hold myself firm! Here is the touch of Death — Where some colour has flown and left a stain on the door! You mock me! I am no fool — wait! There are other reasons to maintain a sense of social shame!
14
'सुटुक्क झट्ट आ !' भनी रुमाल फर्फराउँछयौ ? कटाक्ष तीक्ष्ण लाउँछउौ ? सवाल मौन ल्याउँछयौ ? यहीं छ द्धार, देख्छु है ! ए पर्ख ! क्यै निरख्छु है ! म देख्छु छूत मृत्युको ! त्यो चोइटो खसेछ है !
“Come quickly, come now!” — do you flutter your handkerchief? Do you cast sharp glances? Does your question bring silence? Here is the door — I can see it! Wait — let me look closely! I see the touch of Death upon you! That shadow has fallen!
15
यहाँ त मृत्यु देखिनै हुँदैन रे तिमी जहाँ ! न हाँस ! कालको कथा कता कता सुनिन्छ है ! तिमी नजीक काल के ? म कालसाथ लड्दछु ! तिमी भिडाउ सुन्दरी ? म मृत्यु साथ भिड्दछु !
Where you are, death should not be seen at all! Don't laugh! The story of time is heard faintly somewhere! What is Death doing near you? I will fight with Death! Will you engage me, beautiful one? I will wrestle with Death!
16
उषा थिइन्, जली मरिन्! सुवर्ण–साँझ अल्पिइन् न आँशु, हुन्, न तारका, वितान झल्झलाउँछ ! न देखिने खुँडा लिने मलाई क्वै सताउँछ ! कुचाल मार्छ सुस्तरी ममा प्रहार गर्न ए !
Dawn was — she burned and died! The golden evening faded — No tears, no stars — yet the canopy still glitters! An invisible force with a blade torments me! Destruction moves slowly, striking at me from within!
17
प्रसूनसाथ रोइनौ ? निशाग्रटधोइनौदृष्टि? र पुत्तली पखेरुका सुवर्ण–धूल छोइनौ ? सुचारु साँझ जल्दथिन्, तिनी अगाडि रोइनौ ? गलेर चाँदनी कणी, जली, ढली, थपोइनौ ?
Did you not weep with the flowers? Did you not wash your eyes in night dew? Did you not touch the golden dust of butterfly wings? When the beautiful evening burned, did you not weep before it? As moonlight melted, burned and dissolved — did you not tremble?
18
म मार्छु मृत्यु सुन्दरी ! म क्रोधमा छु ! मार्न द्यौ ! विनाश चाल मार्छ रे ! म रोक्छु गर्व झर्न द्यौ ! म फूल झर्न दिन्न रे ! म रङ्ग उड्न दिन्न रे ! अहा ! यो मुस्कुरी पला अमोल हिँडन दिन्न रे !
I will kill Death, O beautiful one! I am in rage! Let me strike! Destruction plays its move! I block it — let pride fall away! I will not let the flower fall! I will not let the colour fade! Ah! This priceless smiling moment — I will not let it walk away!
19
तँ वैंश ! अड ! नमर् ! नमर् ! म गाउँला सदा ! सदा ! सुरा न सिद्धियोस् परी ! नशा भरी, र प्यास होस्! तँ झ्यालको न नाश होस्! तँ दृष्टि नित्य दास हो ! झकाइ, यो अटूट होस् ! तिमी, सदा निमन्त्रणा !
You, youth! Stand firm! Don't die! Don't die! I will sing forever! Forever! Let the wine not be exhausted, O fairy! Let there be intoxication and thirst! Let your window not be destroyed! Let your gaze be forever enslaved to beauty! This bond — let it be unbroken! You — always an invitation!
20
ए चन्द्रमा ! कला नफेर्! तँ मेघ हे ! नजा नघेर्! तँ पोज बस्! तँ ओठ जी ! 'मुसुक्क !' मर्त्यता नवर् ! तँ नूर हो स्थिरप्रभा ! यहाँ छ –कामिनीशिखा धधग धधग ज्वलन्त जो, तँ चित्त ! नित्य धग्धगा !
O moon — do not change your phase! O cloud — do not go, do not gather! Hold your pose! Let those lips live! That gentle smile — let mortality not consume it! You are light, steady radiance! Here burns the flame of womanhood — Blazing, blazing, burning — O heart! Burn forever!
21
म वीर हुन्छु प्रेयसी ! शिंगार शस्त्रले तिमी ! पठाउ काल युद्धमा ! चढाउ अश्व, कल्पना ! अरण्यमा विशाल त्यो जहाँ गएर जानकी दिएर रामवाहुमा प्रचण्ड शक्ति मुस्किइन्!
I become a warrior, O beloved! You — with beauty as your weapon! Send Death to battle! Mount imagination as your steed! In that vast forest where Janaki (Sita) once went — She smiled, having given fierce strength into Rama's arms!
22
निचोर सृष्टिकी तिमी सजीव कामिनी बन्यौ ! तिमी, ममा छ विश्वको त फ्याक, फ्याक रे मुटु ! तिमी, म साथ चल्छ यो ! तिमी, म साथ फुल्छ यो ! हिमाल सम्झ पार्वती ! रसालकुञ्ज, राधिके !
You are the very essence of creation, a living divine feminine! In you, in me — the world's heart beats, beats! With you, with me — this walks! With you, with me — this blooms! Think of yourself as Parvati of the Himalayas! As Radha of the lush grove!
23
अचम्म ! छौ बजारमा ? यहाँ कसोरि छौ तिमी ? विशाल सृष्टि–वल्लरी प्रफल्ल एक पुष्प ए ? म सोध्छु ए ! विचित्रकी तरङ्ग ! वृत्ति–बावली ! मभित्र बज्छ बाँसुरी, मिहीन औ पुरानिया !
Wonder! You are in the marketplace? How are you here of all places? Are you one blooming flower of the vast vine of creation? I ask! O wave of strangeness! O mind gone wild with longing! Inside me plays a flute — fine and ancient!
24
न तौल अङ्गुली तिमी ! दशैं त मूल्य हो भनी ! हजार ज्यान अर्पिने विलासदास सामुमा ! अहो ! कसोरि सस्तियौ ? तिमी ? लसुन, प्याज झैं ! छ सानु रश्मि जो यहाँ, चढाउलाई हाँक खै ?
You cannot be weighed on fingers — yet they say everything has a price! Before a thousand pleasure-slaves who offer themselves! Alas! How did you become so cheap? You? Like garlic and onion! There is a small ray of light here — who will call it worthy of worship?
25
बुझें ! बनेर व्यङ्ग्यकी विशालता तिमी बस्यौ बजारमा समाजको, गिज्याइ आजको मुख ? जहाँ छ साँढे स्वार्थको सुचारु–पुष्पभक्षक ! जहाँ छ राज्य मासुको र आँशुको जलाशय !
I understand now! You sit as the vast embodiment of irony — In the marketplace of society, mocking the face of today? Where the bull of selfishness devours beautiful flowers! Where flesh rules and tears form a reservoir!
26
छ कीट लुब्ध पुष्पमा ! विनाशसार प्यार छ ! ठगी, अपूर्व रुपको ! र नोटको बजार छ ! चुरो किनिन्छ मानवी ! गरीबमाथि मार छ ! जहाँ समाज नामको कुनर्कको मुहार छ !
There are insects greedy on flowers! Love here is made of destruction! Deception of extraordinary beauty! And a marketplace of currency notes! Human essence itself is bought and sold! The poor are crushed! Where something called society wears the face of a corrupt sun!
27
अहा ! बनेर व्यङ्ग्यकी प्रदीपिका जल्यौ तिमी ! तिमी मनुष्य–मातृका ! तिमी सुधा ! तिमी क्षुधा ! विशाल विश्वदीपिका तिमी ! तमोमयी तृषा ?
Ah! You burn as the lamp of irony itself! You are the mother-script of humanity! You are nectar! You are hunger! You are the vast lamp of the world! A thirst filled with darkness?
28
यो लात हो विरिञ्चीमा ! यो सृष्टिमाथि लाञ्छना ! निसर्गको प्रवञ्चना ! यो दागदीप आजको ? विशाल व्यङ्ग्य, व्यङ्ग्य हो ! समाजको समाजको !
This is a kick at Brahma himself! A slur upon all creation! A betrayal of nature! Is this today's stained lamp? A vast irony — irony it is! Of society! Of society!
29
प्रकोप–मूर्छिता चिता ! कराल सुन्दरी तिमी ! निशुम्भ, शुम्भ डाक्तछयौ ? प्रचण्ड, चण्ड, मुण्ड के ? ए व्यङ्ग्य ! व्यङ्ग्य नै बनी गिगिल्ल, हाँस झ्यालमा ! म बुझ्न सक्छु कालिके ! ल नाच ए भयङ्करा !
A funeral pyre fainted with fury! You are a terrible beauty! Do you summon Nishumbha and Shumbha? Prachand, Chand, Mund? O irony — becoming irony itself — laugh giggling at the window! I can understand you, O Kalika! Then dance, O terrible one!
30
म मार्छु मृत्यु सुन्दरी ! म क्रोधमा छु ! मार्न द्यौ ! म फूल झर्न दिन्न रे ! म रङ्ग उड्न दिन्न रे ! तँ वैंश ! अड ! नमर् ! नमर् ! म गाउँला सदा ! सदा ! झकाइ, यो अटूट होस् ! तिमी, सदा निमन्त्रणा !
I will kill Death, O beautiful one! I am in rage! Let me strike! I will not let the flower fall! I will not let the colour fade! You, youth! Stand firm! Don't die! Don't die! I will sing forever! This bond — let it be unbroken! You — always an invitation!

Verse by Verse Analysis

The analysis below quotes the original Nepali lines, gives the English translation directly beneath each quotation, and then unpacks what Devkota was doing philosophically, historically, and literarily in each movement of the poem.

Verses 1 and 2: The World Drunk on Gold, and the Goddess at the Window

छ प्राप्तिको सुरा शरद् ! सुखोत्सवी छ मानव ! फली, फुकी, धनीधरा सुनौलिएर मुस्किइन्! सुधा–उरीज सप्किंदी ! स्वभावमस्त ! मुस्कुरी ! ए सुन्दरी ? रसा सरी रुमाल फर्फराउँछयौ ?
रसाल भार रेशमी विचित्र चित्र रेखिए ! बन्यौ कि फूलले तिमी ? सुगन्ध ली हवा बहे ! रङ्गीन ज्यान झ्यालकी ! बजारबीचकी उषा ! उपास्दथें म अम्बिका ! झसङ्ग झैं यहाँ भएँ !

Autumn is the wine of achievement! Mankind is festive with pleasure! The rich earth blossomed, bloomed, gilded itself and smiled! Nectar-drops sparkling! Nature-intoxicated! Smiling! O beautiful one — do you flutter your handkerchief like the earth herself? Your silken burden is woven with strange and gorgeous patterns! Were you made of flowers? The wind carries your fragrance away! Colourful soul at the window! Dawn in the middle of the marketplace! I used to worship Ambika the goddess — now I am startled to find her here!

Devkota does not open this poem where we might expect him to, at the woman's window. He opens at the world around her. The year is the 1940s. Nepal sits sealed inside itself under the Rana oligarchy, a system of hereditary prime ministers that had turned the country into private property. The ruling class celebrates its acquisitions while the vast majority of Nepali life unfolds in quiet deprivation. Into this picture Devkota paints an autumn drunk on getting and having, an earth that has bloomed golden not with joy but with the satisfaction of commerce. And then, against all this hollow festivity, a woman appears at a window. She is a courtesan. She is fluttering a handkerchief. His first instinct is not condemnation, not pity, not the moral calculation that every other literary or religious voice of his era would have performed automatically. It is wonder.

रङ्गीन ज्यान झ्यालकी ! बजारबीचकी उषा ! उपास्दथें म अम्बिका ! झसङ्ग झैं यहाँ भएँ !

Colourful soul at the window! Dawn in the middle of the marketplace! I used to worship Ambika the goddess — now I am startled to find her here!

He asks whether she moves like the earth herself, whether her gesture carries the same natural ease as the whole blooming world. Devkota sees her not as a prostitute but as a flower, as a work of creation, as a human soul pressed into impossible circumstances. The shock in these opening verses comes from the contrast between her intrinsic worth and what the world has done with that worth, and then arrives the most seismic line in Nepali literature.

He used to worship the goddess Ambika in her proper place, the temple, with the proper rituals on the proper days. Now he stands before a marketplace window and he is startled, genuinely startled, because the goddess is right here. This was not metaphor to Devkota. It was genuine recognition. In the Rana era, to say that the sacred lived in the courtesan was to say that the temple's monopoly on the divine was fraudulent. Religious and social hierarchies were fused together. The temple was moral architecture. Devkota took a hammer to it with a single line and called the act worship. Today this verse reads as a portrait of late capitalism that Devkota could not have named but absolutely felt. The world is still drunk on acquisition. Beauty is still pressed to a window, commodified and looked past. The handkerchief still flutters.

Verses 3 and 4: She Is Made of Moonlight, He Is Made of String

शरन्निशा तिमी झुकी, झिकी लजालु घूँघट, स्मिति–प्रयोगिनी बनी, जिती सफेद चाँदनी, सितारिंदी उदाउँछयौ ? बनाइ अङ्ग मख्मली ? टुना गरेर चित्तमा चकोरका झिलीमिली ?
म हूँ सितार स्नायुको, बजीरहेछु झन्किँदै ! समुद्र आज हो बडो छचल्किँदो जुनेलिँदै !

In the autumn night you bow, pulling aside your shy veil, Becoming a practitioner of smiles, outshining the white moonlight, Do you rise scattering stars? Having made your limbs like velvet? Casting a spell in the heart with the glittering of moonbirds? I am the sitar of nerves, playing and resonating! The ocean today is vast, churning and moonlit!

Every moral authority of Devkota's era would have insisted that a courtesan was the opposite of modest, that her very profession was an assault on decency. Devkota refuses this entirely. He describes her as shy. He gives her a veil. He makes her pull it aside with the same bashful gesture as the autumn moon moving behind clouds. Her smile is practiced, yes, she has learned to weaponise it in a world that left her no other weapon, but it defeats moonlight. She rises like a constellation. The moonbird, the chakor, that Devkota places in these verses is one of the oldest images in Sanskrit and Nepali poetry. The chakor cannot stop gazing at the moon. It longs for what it can never fully possess, and the longing itself is the experience of being fully alive. By placing this image here Devkota is already telling us what kind of relationship this is. It is not transaction. It is longing of the most ancient and spiritual kind.

म हूँ सितार स्नायुको, बजीरहेछु झन्किँदै !

I am the sitar of nerves, playing and resonating!

And then he names what he is in response to her. He is a sitar, an instrument built for resonance, not for logic. When she appears at the window, he does not think. He vibrates. The ocean metaphor that closes these verses is enormous in its implication, churning and moonlit simultaneously, beautiful from above and turbulent in its depths. This is the poet's interior state described with perfect precision. In the Sanskrit rasa tradition that Devkota had absorbed, all emotional states were categorised into essences. He floods the dominant rasa of shringara, the rasa of love and beauty, with every other rasa simultaneously, as a moonlit ocean contains every shade of water.

Verses 5 through 8: The Cosmological Argument for Desire

गुलाफतापले रचे विरिञ्चिले मनुष्य कि ? मिलेर राग, वासना, फलिन्, फुलिन् वसुन्धरा ! छ मासुरङ्ग रेशमी लुगा लगाउने चरी ! अनङ्गबागमा बसी अलाप्दछे धनी सुरा!
म मासु–लाञ्छना गरी म पस्छु है चिसो गुफा ! म फूलको हूँ पारखी ! म पुज्छु सृष्टि, सुन्दरी!
म स्वप्निंदो छु उर्वशी, जिलाउँदो छु मेनका ! मलाई दिव्य कामिनीको तिर्सना अनन्त छ ! म मत्तमा ! म मस्तता ! मलाई प्यास सुन्दर ! म अड्छु ! फुल्छु, हेर्छु रे ! मनुष्य पुष्प फर्फर !

Did Brahma fashion mankind with the warmth of roses? Blending passion and desire, the earth bore fruit and blossomed! She is a bird wearing silken clothes the colour of flesh! Sitting in the garden of Desire, she sings her rich melody! I enter this cold cave bearing the stigma of flesh! I am a connoisseur of flowers! I worship creation, O beautiful one! I am the dreaming Urvashi, I am the life-giving Menaka! My longing for the divine feminine is endless! I am intoxication! I am ecstasy! I thirst for beauty! I stand firm! I bloom, I gaze! A human flower, fluttering!

This is where the poem moves from personal testimony to philosophy. Devkota asks whether the creator himself used the warmth of roses, the mingling of passion and longing, as the actual material of human creation, and he means this literally.

गुलाफतापले रचे विरिञ्चिले मनुष्य कि ? मिलेर राग, वासना, फलिन्, फुलिन् वसुन्धरा !

Did Brahma fashion mankind with the warmth of roses? Blending passion and desire, the earth bore fruit and blossomed!

The Vedantic tradition that formed him holds that the world emerges from and returns to a single consciousness, that all forms are expressions of one underlying reality. Devkota takes this and makes it radically democratic: if Brahma mixed desire and passion into the clay of humanity, then a woman who embodies desire is not fallen from creation. She is close to its original recipe. She is the bird in the garden of Ananga, the god of desire, singing a melody that belongs to the original score of creation.

Then Devkota names the stigma he carries entering her world. He says he enters bearing the mark of flesh, the social brand of the man who desires. But rather than apologising, he reframes it entirely. He is a parakhi, a connoisseur, someone with genuine discernment, someone who knows the difference between the real and the counterfeit. He worships creation. The cold cave he enters is not the brothel as sinful space. It is the cave of social judgment, dark and icy, where desire is shamed and hidden. He walks into it as a worshipper walks into a temple.

The verses that follow build one of the most extraordinary declarations of embodiment in all of Nepali literary history. Every item in his long list of what he is, the heartbeat of flesh, pain, breath, skin, vision, the answer to light, the adornment of feet, is a physical thing containing something immaterial within it. And then Urvashi and Menaka arrive. In Hindu mythology these apsaras are celestial beings whose beauty interrupted even the meditation of the greatest sages. The tradition told those stories as cautionary tales. Devkota inverts the entire framework: he becomes the beauty himself, becomes the longing, becomes the force the tradition warned against, and calls himself a human flower fluttering in the wind. It is possibly the most tender self-description in all of Nepali literature.

Verses 9 through 12: Krishna, Shiva, and the Law That Cannot Be Repealed

निसर्गको छ ऐन यो ! छ कृष्णलाई चैन यो ! समाधि–मग्न शम्भुको प्रकोपवादको खुशी ! यही त ब्रह्मपाश हो ! र बाँधिने मिठास हो ! म पौडिने तरङ्ग हो ! म मस्तिने सुवास हो !

This is nature's own law! This is Krishna's own peace! The joy of Shambhu's fury even in deepest meditation! This is Brahma's own snare! And the sweetness of being bound! I am the wave that swims! I am the fragrance that intoxicates!

The bloom of the heart at a gesture, the stirring of the chest at a call, Devkota insists this is not weakness or base appetite. It is nature's own law. It is the peace that Krishna himself acknowledged when he played his flute in the Vrindavan moonlight and drew all of creation toward him through sheer beauty and longing. It is the fury hidden inside Shiva's deepest meditation, the creative destruction that exists even in perfect stillness. And then he calls it.

यही त ब्रह्मपाश हो ! र बाँधिने मिठास हो !

This is Brahma's own snare! And the sweetness of being bound!

Rather than lamenting the trap he calls it sweet. Being bound to beauty is sweet. The thirst is not a lack. It is an experience. This is a position of profound philosophical maturity, to understand that what the world calls weakness is the most honest expression of what it means to have been created at all. The wave that swims is one of Devkota's most paradoxical and perfect images. A wave does not swim in the ocean. A wave is swimming. It is movement and immersion simultaneously, with no existence separate from what surrounds it. He is saying that his longing for beauty is not something he experiences from a safe distance. He is made of it.

In the context of 1940s Nepal, where the ascetic ideal was the publicly honoured mode of spiritual life and to want nothing was to be considered everything, these verses were quietly incendiary. Devkota was saying that the man who feels fully, who resonates like a sitar at the presence of beauty, is not spiritually inferior to the man who feels nothing. He is spiritually honest.

Verses 13 through 17: The Touch of Death, and the Poet's Rage

म देख्छु छूत मृत्युको ! त्यो चोइटो खसेछ है ! यहाँ त मृत्यु देखिनै हुँदैन रे तिमी जहाँ ! तिमी नजीक काल के ? म कालसाथ लड्दछु ! तिमी भिडाउ सुन्दरी ? म मृत्यु साथ भिड्दछु !
म मार्छु मृत्यु सुन्दरी ! म क्रोधमा छु ! मार्न द्यौ ! म फूल झर्न दिन्न रे ! म रङ्ग उड्न दिन्न रे ! अहा ! यो मुस्कुरी पला अमोल हिँडन दिन्न रे !

I see the touch of Death upon you! That shadow has fallen! Where you are, death should not be seen at all! What is Death doing near you? I will fight with Death! Will you engage me, beautiful one? I will wrestle with Death! I will kill Death, O beautiful one! I am in rage! Let me strike! I will not let the flower fall! I will not let the colour fade! Ah! This priceless smiling moment — I will not let it walk away!

This is the emotional pivot of the entire poem, and it arrives with the force of a cold wind through an open door. The poet has been approaching her in ecstasy, building his philosophical case for beauty's divinity, and then he sees it. Something on her body, in her eyes, around her doorway, is marked by death. The shadow has already fallen on the door.

म देख्छु छूत मृत्युको ! त्यो चोइटो खसेछ है !

I see the touch of Death upon you! That shadow has fallen!

In the context of Devkota's era this almost certainly refers to disease. Syphilis was devastatingly common among sex workers in mid-century Nepal, where there was essentially no medical infrastructure available to women in her position. But Devkota does not write about disease. He writes about Kaal, Death itself, the great abstraction, the force that ends everything beautiful. And his response is not to turn away in horror or in the relief of someone who has found a moral reason to leave. His response is rage. Where you are, death should not be seen at all. This is not a sentimental protest. It is a theological statement. If she is Ambika, if she is the flame of creation, then death has no jurisdiction over her doorstep.

He then asks her a series of questions that constitute the most heartbreaking passage in the poem. Did you not weep with the flowers? Did you not touch the golden dust of butterfly wings? Did you not stand before a burning evening and weep because it was ending? He is asking whether she has been fully present to the world's beauty, whether grief and radiance have moved through her the way they move through him. This is not the question a customer asks a commodity. It is the question a poet asks a soul.

And then the poem erupts. The declaration that he will kill Death, that he will not let the flower fall or the colour fade, that this priceless smiling moment will not be allowed to walk away, this is Devkota in full Romantic fury. He has understood what Keats understood, that beauty does not survive without someone willing to fight for it, and he positions himself as that fighter. In Rana Nepal this was political whether he framed it that way or not. A system that extinguishes beauty and possibility and human flourishing is a system that deserves a poet's full, undiminished rage.

Verses 18 through 23: From Sita's Forest to a Garlic Stall

अरण्यमा विशाल त्यो जहाँ गएर जानकी दिएर रामवाहुमा प्रचण्ड शक्ति मुस्किइन्!
हिमाल सम्झ पार्वती ! रसालकुञ्ज, राधिके ! अचम्म ! छौ बजारमा ? यहाँ कसोरि छौ तिमी ?
अहो ! कसोरि सस्तियौ ? तिमी ? लसुन, प्याज झैं ! छ सानु रश्मि जो यहाँ, चढाउलाई हाँक खै ?

In that vast forest where Janaki (Sita) once went — She smiled, having given fierce strength into Rama's arms! Think of yourself as Parvati of the Himalayas! As Radha of the lush grove! Wonder! You are in the marketplace? How are you here of all places? Alas! How did you become so cheap? You? Like garlic and onion! There is a small ray of light here — who will call it worthy of worship?

The Sita reference is exact and devastating. Sita is taken to the forest against her will, exiled by the world's judgment of her purity, subjected to trials she never deserved and never asked for. And yet in the forest she gave Rama the strength to fight. She was the source of his power even in her suffering, perhaps especially in her suffering. Devkota draws the parallel without spelling it out because he trusts his reader: this courtesan, also exiled by the world's judgment, also surviving in a forest of social disapproval and impossible circumstance, is also a source of fierce strength. She is Parvati. She is Radha. She is creation's finest flower. And then comes one of the most searing lines in the poem, arriving with the violence of a price tag slapped on a painting.

अहो ! कसोरि सस्तियौ ? तिमी ? लसुन, प्याज झैं !

Alas! How did you become so cheap? You? Like garlic and onion!

Garlic and onion. The woman who is moon-carved, who is Ambika and the vast lamp of the world, is being sold beside root vegetables. This is not hyperbole. This was the actual economic reality of sex work in 1940s Kathmandu, and it remains the economic reality in too many places today. Devkota's fury is not the fury of the moralist who is offended by the transaction. It is the fury of the worshipper who watches what he has correctly identified as sacred being treated as perishable goods.

There is a small ray of light here, he says. Who will call it worthy of worship? The answer, implied by the entire poem and by the century that has followed it, is that almost no one will. Only the poet who has the capacity for genuine astonishment. Devkota was a man who wept in public, who gave away money he did not have, who worked himself to tuberculosis and died at forty-nine. He was not performing sensitivity. He was built for it. And in a world run on transaction, a man built for genuine feeling is as out of place as a courtesan in a garlic stall.

Verses 24 through 28: The Lamp of Irony and the Corrupt Sun

बुझें ! बनेर व्यङ्ग्यकी विशालता तिमी बस्यौ बजारमा समाजको, गिज्याइ आजको मुख ? जहाँ छ साँढे स्वार्थको सुचारु–पुष्पभक्षक ! जहाँ छ राज्य मासुको र आँशुको जलाशय !
जहाँ समाज नामको कुनर्कको मुहार छ !
अहा ! बनेर व्यङ्ग्यकी प्रदीपिका जल्यौ तिमी ! तिमी मनुष्य–मातृका ! तिमी सुधा ! तिमी क्षुधा ! विशाल विश्वदीपिका तिमी ! तमोमयी तृषा ?

I understand now! You sit as the vast embodiment of irony — In the marketplace of society, mocking the face of today? Where the bull of selfishness devours beautiful flowers! Where flesh rules and tears form a reservoir! Where something called society wears the face of a corrupt sun! Ah! You burn as the lamp of irony itself! You are the mother-script of humanity! You are nectar! You are hunger! You are the vast lamp of the world! A thirst filled with darkness?

The poem undergoes a transformation here that many readers miss because they come to it through the shorter, more famous excerpts. Devkota pulls back to the widest possible philosophical lens and makes an identification that reframes everything that came before. She is not just a beautiful woman he sees with divine eyes. She is irony itself made flesh. She is Vyang, the embodiment of the gap between what the world claims to value and what it actually does with that value. And then arrives one of the most powerful kennings in Nepali literary history.

जहाँ समाज नामको कुनर्कको मुहार छ !

Where something called society wears the face of a corrupt sun!

Kunarka, the corrupt sun. A sun that should illuminate and warm and enable life but has instead gone rotten, burning without nourishing, blinding without revealing. This is society under the Rana system seen without any protective distance. It is also a diagnosis of any society that puts a price on beauty, that buys and sells human essence, that crushes the poor and calls the resulting arrangement by the name of order.

He then names her in rapid succession as Vyangya Pradipika, the lamp of irony. She burns against the darkness of hypocrisy not because she chose this role but because the world placed her in it and she has no choice but to burn. She is the mother-script of humanity, the original text from which all human stories are written. She is nectar and she is hunger simultaneously, because she is the thing that sustains and the thing that the world has refused to be sustained by. This is the most complete philosophical identification in the poem, and it arrives in a rush, three identities in three lines, each one expanding outward from the personal to the cosmic.

Verses 29 through 34: Kali Dances, the Poet Breaks, and Everything Is Asked

प्रकोप–मूर्छिता चिता ! कराल सुन्दरी तिमी ! ए व्यङ्ग्य ! व्यङ्ग्य नै बनी गिगिल्ल, हाँस झ्यालमा ! म बुझ्न सक्छु कालिके ! ल नाच ए भयङ्करा !

A funeral pyre fainted with fury! You are a terrible beauty! O irony — becoming irony itself — laugh giggling at the window! I can understand you, O Kalika! Then dance, O terrible one!

Now Devkota invokes Kali, and this invocation is the most electrifying moment in the poem. The terrible goddess who dances on Shiva's chest, who wears skulls, who is drunk on the blood of demons she has destroyed, this is who he sees at the window. Her laughter is not the laughter of the fallen woman that polite society condescends to. It is the laughter of Kali who has seen through every pretense and finds the hypocrisy of the world genuinely funny in the most terrifying way. A woman who has survived what she has survived and is still laughing is either broken or she is Kali. Devkota insists on Kali. And then.

म बुझ्न सक्छु कालिके ! ल नाच ए भयङ्करा !

I can understand you, O Kalika! Then dance, O terrible one!

He tells her to dance. He says he understands her. These are among the most radical words any Nepali poet of his era put on a page. Not I forgive you. Not I pity you. Not I will rescue you. I understand you. And then: dance your terrible dance.

The verses that follow contain the poem's most honestly conflicted passage, where the poet's own fear and desire and social conditioning all speak at once, calling her names that belong to the voices society has planted inside him. That he names these voices and then overrides them, that he ends even his most agitated confrontation by calling her Urvashi, the celestial apsara, tells us everything about where the poem ultimately stands. The helplessness is his. The poison is the world's. She is still the divine beneath it all.

The poem then asks whether she will abandon her deceptive role, the role the world assigned her, and wear her true form, and whether she will run alongside him through age after age, through kalpa after kalpa. This is a marriage proposal from the deepest register of the Romantic tradition, not for a season but for cosmic time. And it acknowledges that the transformation must be mutual: he cannot simply worship her and leave her at the window. Something has to change in the world that put her there.

Final Verses: The Utopian Vision and the Broken Ending

तँ वैंश ! अड ! नमर् ! नमर् ! म गाउँला सदा ! सदा ! झकाइ, यो अटूट होस् ! तिमी, सदा निमन्त्रणा !
जगत् पखाल्न शक्दथ्यौ ! हिलो भर ! सडी मर !

You, youth! Stand firm! Don't die! Don't die! I will sing forever! This bond — let it be unbroken! You — always an invitation! You could have washed the world! Fill it with mud instead! Rot and die!

The ending of this poem is its most misunderstood passage and the most important one to read with full attention. After the utopian vision, love as a Himalayan home, the moon washing away the stain of commerce, society finally crossing the river together, the poem collapses into something raw and unresolved. The poet has offered everything. He has extended the full philosophical, spiritual, and emotional case for her dignity and her divine nature. And the world, the system, the marketplace, the corrupt sun, has not changed. She is still at the window. The shadow is still on the door.

जगत् पखाल्न शक्दथ्यौ ! हिलो भर ! सडी मर !

You could have washed the world! Fill it with mud instead! Rot and die!

The savage final words are not Devkota's verdict on the woman. They are the sound of a man who has exhausted himself against a wall that does not move. He says she could have washed the world. This is the highest thing the poem says about her, and he says it in the moment of his own despair. You had within you the capacity to cleanse what is corrupt. The woman who is the lamp of irony and the mother-script of humanity and the vast light of the world had the power to wash it. Instead the world filled her with its mud. The rot and die is directed at the system as much as at the individual, at the marketplace that made her cheap, at the society wearing the face of a corrupt sun, at the world that put a price on the moon-carved.

Devkota died in 1959 at forty-nine years old of tuberculosis, having spent his life giving his words and his money and his health away. This poem, written in the Panchachamar meter with its insistent driving rhythm, is the fullest expression of what he believed: that beauty is sacred, that the human capacity for feeling is the most important thing we have, that society's greatest crime is what it does to both, and that the poet's job is to say so, in the finest language available, even when it breaks him.

Why This Poem Is Not Finished With Us

Devkota died in 1959 at forty-nine years old. He died of tuberculosis, having spent his adult life giving his words and his money and his health away to a world that was not always sure what to do with a man who felt everything at full volume. He was called the Mahakavi in his lifetime but he died poor. This is not a coincidence. It is the same story the poem tells: the world recognises the value of beauty in the abstract and then charges it garlic prices in the specific.

The poem you have just read in its entirety is not the poem most people know. Most people know the excerpts, the first flush of astonishment, the declaration that he is the heartbeat of flesh, the rage against death, the plea that the window not be destroyed. These are the parts that fit on a stage, the parts that Bhushita Vasistha's extraordinary voice has carried into concert halls and into the hearts of a new generation of Nepali listeners. But the full poem is something more disturbing and more honest than its highlights. It moves from worship to Kali to broken prayer to the devastating final command, and it does all of this without ever abandoning the position it started from. She is still the goddess at the end. The world is still the corrupt sun. Nothing has been resolved. Everything has been named.

What Devkota understood, and what this poem makes impossible to forget, is that the commodification of beauty is not an accident of capitalism or of any particular political system. It is the default setting of any society that runs on transaction rather than recognition. The default is to assign a price. The radical act is to assign a meaning. Devkota assigned meaning with the full force of his philosophical and literary inheritance, and the meaning he assigned was divine.

The woman at the window is still there. She is in every city, in every marketplace, in every system that puts a price on human beings and calls the resulting arrangement by the name of society. And the question the poem asks, how did someone so beautiful, so sensitive, so alive, end up here, is still the most important question a writer can ask. Not because the answer changes anything immediately, but because the asking refuses to let the world forget what it has done.

Devkota's final words to her are savage and tender in the same breath: you could have washed the world. This is not blame. It is grief. It is the grief of a man who saw clearly what was being wasted and could not stop it with his poetry alone. The poem ends in that grief. But the poem also exists, which means the grief was productive, the seeing was real, the worship was genuine. And somewhere, in every generation of Nepali readers who encounter this poem for the first time, the shock of recognition arrives again: how is she here, in the market? And for a moment, before the world closes in again, she is Ambika. She is dawn in the middle of the marketplace. She is always an invitation.
